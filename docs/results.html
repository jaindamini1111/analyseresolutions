AnalyseResolutions Results


Introduction

This page provides a partial visual walk-through of the tool's processing and of the resulting outputs generated during the case study. The case study was involved in exploring methods alternative to editing for the digital opening of a vast archival series. More specifically, it focussed on the 1740 document of the Resolutions of the States General and explored the use and usefulness of visual information from the material text represented in the digital facsimile for the document's digital opening.


Image Analysis

Image analysis was carried out to recognise select details from the intricate structuring systems by which the document had been laid out. When recognised, these details then allow the reconstruction of the document's physical flow, and based on that, the inferring of the document's logical flow and its constituent parts.

The images below visualise some of the aspects of how the tailored tool 'reads' the document's digital facsimile. Figures 1 and 2 show the first two openings of the body of resolutions as they had been captured by the National Archives of the Netherlands, and figures 3 and 4 visualise part of the processing on top of the original images. Generated XML Source

Transcription is among the first steps in editorial work. Here, the aim was to generate a source text that was as unriddled as possible of OCR errors, as well as one that would include useful high-level structural information derived from the document's images. Towards these aims, the work applied OCR in a tailored and critical manner: (1) the OCR engine was trained to read the document's fonts (note: the tool also makes use of a training file published by the eMOP project); (2) the OCR process was branched in three ways according to the logical roles of the content in the input images as segmented and recognised by preceding stage of image analysis; (3) the language resources used by the three branches were each tailored for their purposes; and (4) the configuration parameters of Tesseract were adjusted to better suit the material at hand.

In the end, the generated source text even with its many imperfections can still be seen both as a useful additional layer of access to the digital facsimile and to the document's textual content; and, as a starting point for further manual or automated enrichment, such as those that have been explored by the Huygens ING's other pilots (see Sluijter et al. 2016).

The small print:

  • OCR Engine. The OCR engine in use was Tesseract v3.05. The font training, language resources and configuration parameters used were highly tailored.
  • Evaluation. The results were evaluated and the reports generated with the ocrevalUAtion tool.
  • Type. The OCR resource use is branched in three-ways according to the logical role of the input.
  • CER. Character error rate.
  • WER. Word error rate.
  • On-the-fly regularisation. The regularisations include correcting for some of the repeating textual structures; for example, 'PRAESENTIBUS' and names of days and months in beginning sections of sessions.
  • Post-processing. The post processing was carried out with a computer-assisted tailored tool, which tries to (1) address systematic errors (e.g. misrecognition of the letter j in place of a comma); and (2) leverage the internal consistency of the document (e.g. if the type 'Brantzenborgh' is a high-frequency item, the tool tries to identify and collapse close by non-dictionary items such as 'Btantzenhorgh' into it). (Note: Only little work has gone into this yet.)
  • Ground truth. The manually transcribed ground truth sets are quite small: for example, starting sections have 386 lines (386/~5591 = ~6.90%) and resumptions and resolutions have 624 lines (624/~83356 = ~0.75%). The sets were used to guide the iterative improvement of the performance.
  • Reality. While the metrics should be quite representative, in reality the OCR output's results are both variable and slightly worse in total than the numbers suggest. The document includes occasional segments where the results are extremely poor (e.g. because of bleedthrough/showthrough and its effects in adaptive binarisation) and segments in other languages where the current language resources can only do damage. The document also includes few tables of figures for which no specialised processing was devised.
